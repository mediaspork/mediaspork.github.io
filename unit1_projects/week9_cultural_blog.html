Is this a good question?

July 6, 2014



As a developer it is extremely important to know how to work with your peers and colleagues. During my short period of time as a developer, I have spent a great deal of time both asking and answering questions. Through this experience I know how valuable it is to know how to ask and respond to well thought out questions.

When trying to answer what is a good question I think that there are two different viewpoints from which we need to view the question: internally and externally.

During the next nine weeks ahead of me at Dev Bootcamp, I am sure that I will experience many distressing situations. In order to stay mentally healthy, it is beneficial to manage your stress. So what do those thoughts say about me, or mean to me? I have found that it is helpful to ask the following questions:

  • Is this thought a fact, or just a thought?
  • What am I reacting to? What am I giving this situation?
  • Is there another way of looking at it?
  • What would someone else make of it? It’s often helpful to think about a particular person who you respect, and imagine what they would think about that thought, what meaning they would give it, and how they would react to it.
  • What advice would I give someone else (with this thought in this situation)?
  • Is my reaction in proportion to the actual event?
  • How important is this really? How important will it be in a year’s time?
  • What evidence is there that this thought is true?
  • What evidence is there that this thought isn’t true (partly or totally)?
  • What would be a more balanced way of looking at it?
  • What is the bigger picture?


Keeping these thoughts and questions in my mind while both answering questions and dealing with stress will prove to be very useful over the coming weeks.

Dealing with questions internally is important, but we still need to know what makes a good question. There are a few things that we can keep in mind when formulating and answering questions:

Avoid questions that require a single, direct answer. This will be useful at times, but questions like these will discourage many pupils from responding, because they will be thinking: “There is one right answer to this and I don’t want to be seen to get it wrong.”

Use questions that invite pupils to talk about what they think. This can help to gather information in a broad way without putting too much pressure on the person answering the question.

Give people time to think. Ask a question and then wait. This will allow people time to analyze the question and consider their answer.

Encourage people to write things down or think out loud. This will help to eliminate stress in their short-term memory and allows you to understand their train of thought.

Keeping all of this in mind, I am ready to start at Dev Bootcamp!
